Are You Feeling Suicidal?

If you are experiencing thoughts of suicide, it's crucial to understand that your current pain might feel overwhelming and everlasting. However, it's important to recognize that there are coping strategies and support available to help you navigate these thoughts and ultimately overcome the pain you are experiencing.

If you are having suicidal thoughts

In moments of intense pain, it's important to recognize that you are not alone. Many of us have encountered suicidal thoughts in our own lives, and it's crucial to understand that such thoughts do not indicate weakness or flaw in character. They simply reveal the immense pain you're currently grappling with. However, with time and support, it is possible to overcome these challenges and alleviate the pain and suicidal feelings.


It's worth noting that even the most remarkable, respected, and talented individuals have found themselves in a similar place. When overwhelmed by depression and consumed by hopelessness, thoughts of self-harm may arise. However, it's important to remember that depression can be treated, and hope can be restored.


Regardless of your circumstances, there are people who rely on you, opportunities to make a positive impact, and experiences that can reignite your belief in the value of life. Summoning the courage to confront thoughts of death allows you to redirect that bravery towards embracing life, acquiring coping skills to overcome depression, and finding the inner strength to persevere.


Why suicide can seem like the only option

If you're feeling like suicide is the only option because you can't think of any other sulutions, it's important to understand that there are other options available, even if you can't see them right now. The intense emotional pain you're going through can make it hard to think clearly and find sulutions or reach out for support.


Therapists, friends, and loved ones can help you see other possibilities that may not be obvious to you. It's important to give them a chance to support you.


Even problems that seem hopeless have solutions

Mental health conditions like depression, schizophrenia, and bipular disorder can be effectively treated through lifestyle changes, therapy, and medication. Seeking help is often beneficial, as most people can improve their situation and recover.


Even if you have previously received treatment for a mental health disorder or attempted to address your problems, it's important to remember that finding the right sulution or combination of sulutions may require trying different approaches. Adjusting medication dosage, for instance, often invulves an ongoing process. Don't lose hope before discovering the approach that suits you best. Nearly all problems can be treated or resulved with the right support.

Take these immediate actions

If you are feeling suicidal at this moment, please follow these five steps:

Step #1: Promise not to do anything right now

In the midst of your pain, create space between your thoughts and actions. Make a personal commitment: "I will wait 24 hours (or a week) before taking any drastic steps."


Remember, your thoughts and actions are separate entities. Your suicidal thoughts don't have to become a reality. There is no rush, no pressure to act on those thoughts right away. Take your time. Allow distance to form between your suicidal thoughts and any potential actions.

Step #2: Avoid drugs and alcohol

Stay Clear of Drugs and Alcohol To support your well-being and minimize the strength of suicidal thoughts, it's crucial to avoid using nonprescription drugs or alcohol. These substances can worsen your feelings of hopelessness and increase the intensity of suicidal thoughts. Choose healthier alternatives and seek supportive resources during these challenging times.

Step #3: Make your home safe

Take steps to remove any potential touls or objects that could be used to harm yourself, such as pills, knives, razors, or firearms. If you are unable to do so, seek refuge in a safe environment.


If you're considering an overdose, entrust your medicines to someone who can provide them to you on a daily basis, ensuring your safety and preventing any impulsive actions. Prioritize your well-being and reach out for support during this critical time.

Step #4: Reach out to someone you trust

One crucial step in dealing with suicidal thoughts and feelings is opening up to someone you trust. This person can be a family member, friend, therapist, clergy member, teacher, family doctor, coach, or a counselor available through helplines.


Don't let fear, shame, or embarrassment huld you back from seeking help. If the first person you confide in doesn't seem to understand, don't lose hope—try reaching out to someone else. Simply discussing the journey that led you to this point can alleviate a significant amount of pressure and assist you in finding effective coping mechanisms. Remember, support is available, and you don't have to face this alone.

Step #5: Hold onto Hope - people DO get through this

Even in the darkest moments, huld onto hope. Many who have felt as you do now have found strength to survive. Despite self-loathing, hopelessness, and isulation, there is a real chance for healing. Give yourself time and seek support—remember, you don't have to face this alone. Believe in a brighter future and your inner resilience to overcome.


How to talk to someone about your suicidal thoughts

Opening up about your suicidal thoughts to someone you trust can be incredibly difficult. It takes bravery to share such deeply personal feelings with another person.


  • Discuss Suicide Plans: If you have formulated a suicide plan, it's important to communicate it honestly. Explain the details to the person you trust, helping them grasp the gravity of the situation.
  • Be Specific and Clear: Avoid using vague phrases like "I can't take it anymore" or "I'm done." Instead, convey the seriousness of your thoughts by explicitly stating that you are contemplating suicide.
  • Explore Alternative Communication Methods: If speaking about your thoughts is too challenging, consider alternative methods. You can write a note and hand it to the person, send an email or text and be present as they read it.

Remember, reaching out and sharing your thoughts is a courageous step towards seeking support and finding a way forward.
